Core idea

Biology treated like software architecture.

Instead of static molecules, programmable biological drugs operate more like adaptive programs: they read state, run rules, and trigger an output only when the system context is right.

Module 01

Sense

Therapeutic systems detect disease signatures such as proteins, metabolites, or gene-expression states before making any move.

Module 02

Compute

Logic gates and control circuits evaluate whether conditions are jointly satisfied, suppressed, or delayed.

Module 03

Respond

Only after validation does the payload fire: a signal, an edit, an immune instruction, or a protein output.

Five layers

A systems map for biological programming.

The page flow mirrors an engineering stack: circuits, transient RNA control, precise editing, living-cell platforms, and closed-loop gene networks.

01

Genetic circuits

Logic gate patterns that reduce off-target activation by requiring specific combinations of signals.

02

RNA programming

mRNA, siRNA, and riboswitches enable flexible, temporary control without permanently rewriting the genome.

03

CRISPR editing

Guide-driven genomic find-and-replace for correction, insertion, deletion, or fine-tuned base editing.

04

Living cell therapies

Engineered CAR-T, NK, or macrophage systems become active therapeutic agents with embedded decision rules.

05

Sensor, processor, and actuator modules work as a self-regulating therapeutic program.

End-to-end walkthrough

Case study: how this prototype behaves as a programmable therapeutic storyboard.

Four scenarios walk from clinic-like inputs through sense → compute → respond, then show how each step maps onto this page’s modules (research cards, evidence strip, design intent). Numbers are illustrative, not trial data.

You can read any scenario alone. Together they show a positive activation path (A–C), a withheld-output path (D), and how the same UI skeleton would accept real biomarker traces, protocol tables, and citations without rearranging sections.

A

Solid tumor: dual-antigen coincidence before CAR-T activation

Synthetic immunology · positive path

A patient with refractory ovarian carcinoma is enrolled on a concept program using autologous T cells engineered with a two-input AND gate: surface antigen A, surface antigen B, and a tumor-protease-cleaved “arming” peptide must align before full CD3ζ signaling is engaged.

  1. 1
    Sense

    Baseline staging and input streams

    Imaging documents disease distribution; blood and ascites show soluble antigen A above a pre-specified floor. Biopsy RNA-seq supports antigen B transcript in tumor epithelium but not in adjacent normal tissue. Pharmacokinetic sampling establishes clearance of lymphodepletion therapy so the engineered product is not competing with residual alemtuzumab.

    On this page: corresponds to Sensor layer in the hero panel and “Input state / biomarker stream” in the architecture diagram. In Research tracks, populate Intervention class and Biological rationale on the synthetic immunology card with these modalities.

  2. 2
    Compute

    Coincidence logic at the cell surface

    After infusion, engineered cells traffic to tumor beds. The circuit requires (A_hi ∧ B_hi ∧ protease_active) within a dwell-time window; if only one antigen dominates, co-receptors remain phosphorylation-limited and the cell stays in a surveillance state with minimal cytokine release.

    On this page: maps to Logic kernel in the hero and “Decision logic / circuit evaluation” in the diagram. The Sense · Compute · Respond cards restate the same contract for a non-specialist audience.

  3. 3
    Respond

    Licensed cytolysis and measurable tumor dynamics

    When the AND condition holds persistently, the construct engages full activation, granzyme release is localized to multi-antigen-positive clusters, and follow-up imaging at week 6 shows partial response by RECIST with declining CA-125.

    On this page: aligns with Therapeutic output in the hero. Under Evidence, you would paste RECIST / CA-125 trajectories into the CONSORT-style table rows and attach the corresponding publication in the citation layer.

  4. 4
    Sense · governance

    Safety readouts close the loop

    CRS grade and ferritin are monitored on a fixed schedule; if grade ≥2 CRS emerges, protocol holds further doses and adds tocilizumab per standard of care. The therapeutic “program” includes an institutional rule layer outside the gene circuit—still part of how the intervention operates in practice.

    On this page: the safety row in the CONSORT scaffold and a footnote column in the same table capture this governance without a new section.

B

Type 1 diabetes: glucose band drives graded insulin transgene output

Closed-loop metabolic · branching

The same prototype page can narrate two branches from one intervention class: aggressive correction when glucose is high, and conservative withholding when glucose falls toward hypoglycemia.

  1. 1
    Sense

    Continuous glucose telemetry as the primary input

    A subcutaneous sensor stream (e.g., 5-minute intervals) feeds a control policy implemented partly in hardware and partly in the genetic program: interstitial glucose is calibrated against capillary checks during the first 72 hours post-implant.

    On this page: the metabolic research card “Intervention class” names the encapsulated or in vivo circuit; “Biological rationale” explains graded output versus bolus insulin.

  2. 2
    Compute

    Branch A — hyperglycemia correction

    When smoothed glucose exceeds the upper band for >30 minutes, transcription of the insulin transgene ramps along a saturating curve; the circuit includes a leak term so basal expression is never strictly zero, avoiding complete metabolic silence.

    On this page: diagram node “Decision logic” can be annotated in a future figure with two branches diverging from one comparator node.

  3. 3
    Compute

    Branch B — hypoglycemia protection

    When glucose crosses below the lower band, the circuit applies rapid negative dominance: transgene output is suppressed within minutes, glucagon rescue remains available per protocol, and logging flags the event for clinician review.

    On this page: a second row group in the CONSORT-style table could separate “efficacy estimand” vs “hypoglycemia avoidance” secondary endpoints without changing layout.

  4. 4
    Respond

    Time-in-range and rescue medication use

    At month 3, CGM exports show increased time-in-range (70–180 mg/dL) versus the patient’s pre-implant baseline; severe hypoglycemia events are zero in the reporting window. Those become the headline outcomes in the evidence strip.

    On this page: figure panel holds CGM ribbon plots; citation layer cites the protocol registration and statistical analysis plan.

C

Personalized neoantigen mRNA: from biopsy to peripheral immune readout

Vaccines · pipeline choreography

This scenario emphasizes steps that are not all biological computing—manufacturing, release testing, and clinical administration—but still fit the same sense/compute/respond narrative for the overall product story.

  1. 1
    Sense

    Tumor and host sequencing define the antigen set

    FFPE tumor and matched normal blood yield exome calls; neoantigen prediction ranks missense variants by HLA binding and RNA support. A clinical tumor board approves the final peptide list encoded into the GMP batch.

    On this page: Plausible indication anchor on the mRNA oncology card names adjuvant or MRD contexts; sequencing is the upstream “sensor” before any drug product exists.

  2. 2
    Compute

    Batch release as a computational QC gate

    Identity, potency (expression in reporter cells), and endotoxin specs must pass before vials ship. That gate is the industrial analogue of a biological AND: “batch ID correct ∧ potency ≥ threshold ∧ safety panel clear.”

    On this page: optional callout box in a future figure could mirror the architecture diagram’s middle node, labeled “QC / release logic.”

  3. 3
    Respond

    Lymph-node delivery and ex vivo T-cell readout

    Intranodal administration on days 0, 14, 28 is followed by apheresis at day 35. ELISpot against pooled peptides shows new IFN-γ spots versus baseline; tetramer staining enriches for CD8 populations specific to two encoded neoepitopes.

    On this page: the evidence table gains rows for immunogenicity secondary endpoints; the figure slot holds ELISpot / tetramer panels with shared numbering to references [3]–[4] in the list.

D

Epigenome editor: when the circuit intentionally does not fire

Negative path · specificity

Programmable therapeutics need a credible off state. Here, dCas-repressor is targeted to a promoter implicated in a hepatic malignancy, but healthy hepatocytes express a low-level decoy transcript that fails the fold-change threshold.

  1. 1
    Sense

    Single-cell RNA in adjacent normal shows sub-threshold target

    Preclinical organoid data show target promoter activity in tumoroids but not in matched normal organoids when normalized to housekeeping expression.

    On this page: the epigenome editor card’s Biological rationale cites reversibility; this step supplies the quantitative “why off-target tissue is ignored.”

  2. 2
    Compute

    Fold-change AND gate returns false in hepatocytes

    In simulation and rodent studies, the ratio of tumor to normal promoter activity never crosses the AND threshold simultaneously with guide occupancy metrics, so the repressor module remains disengaged in healthy tissue.

    On this page: diagram “circuit evaluation” is where a future pathway figure would show the failed branch in muted styling.

  3. 3
    Respond

    No clinical output change where logic is false

    Liver enzymes remain stable; tumor-specific serum marker declines only after a separate combination agent is introduced—attributable to combination therapy, not spurious epigenetic silencing in normal liver.

    On this page: CONSORT table notes column documents “no emergent hepatotoxicity signal”; citations link to IND-enabling tox package summaries.

Using this case study with stakeholders: clinicians can follow scenarios A–D in order; engineers map each step-map line to a component they own (assay, vector, control software, statistics); regulatory writers lift the same headings into the briefing book while the public site keeps the shorter modules above. All paths assume fictional composite patients for illustration.

Research tracks

Prototype cards for active frontier programs.

This section turns the source material's frontier-lab survey into a more product-like research gallery, useful if you want the site to feel both scientific and investable.

Synthetic immunology circuits

Oncology
Intervention class
Autologous or allogeneic T-cell therapy with synthetic receptor logic (multi-input AND/OR gating on surface antigens and/or protease cleavage).
Biological rationale
Restricts cytolytic or co-stimulatory output to combinatorial tumor microenvironment signatures, aiming to widen therapeutic index versus single-antigen CAR approaches.
Plausible indication anchor
Solid tumors with heterogeneous antigen loss (e.g., ovarian, pancreatic) where multi-antigen coincidence is hypothesized to enrich tumor-specific activation.

Closed-loop insulin systems

Metabolic
Intervention class
In vivo gene circuit or encapsulated cell device encoding glucose-responsive insulin secretion (closed-loop endocrine substitute).
Biological rationale
Continuous sensing of circulating glucose (or proxy metabolites) drives graded transgene output, reducing exposure during euglycemia and hypoglycemia risk versus fixed dosing.
Plausible indication anchor
Type 1 diabetes with labile control or recurrent severe hypoglycemia despite optimized pump/ CGM regimens; adjunct or bridge to transplantation.

Personalized mRNA oncology

Vaccines
Intervention class
Individualized neoantigen-encoding mRNA (or self-amplifying RNA) delivered via LNP or intranodal routes with defined immunostimulant backbone.
Biological rationale
Aligns CD8+ responses to tumor-specific missense frames predicted from exome and HLA typing; manufacturing batch tied to each patient’s mutanome.
Plausible indication anchor
Adjuvant or minimal residual disease settings in high-mutation-burden cancers (e.g., melanoma, MSI-high colorectal) where peripheral T-cell expansion correlates with delayed relapse.

Epigenome editors

Genomics
Intervention class
Dead Cas (dCas) or nuclease-deficient editors fused to transcriptional activators or repressors targeted to promoter or enhancer elements.
Biological rationale
Modulates chromatin accessibility and RNA output without double-strand breaks, offering a titratable, potentially reversible layer versus nuclease editing.
Plausible indication anchor
Monogenic disorders with toxic gain-of-function transcription (e.g., selected neurodevelopmental or hepatic storage phenotypes) or oncology programs silencing oncofetal drivers.

Forward view

A roadmap from AI-designed circuits to biological operating systems.

The long-term narrative remains the strongest part of the concept, so the prototype keeps it as a timeline that escalates from near-term feasibility to speculative platform thinking.

2025–2030

AI-designed genetic circuits

Machine-learning systems help design regulatory architectures faster and with better fit to specific disease contexts.

2030–2038

In vivo reprogramming

Delivery systems evolve so that cell engineering can happen directly inside the body rather than through ex vivo manufacturing.

2032–2040

Distributed therapeutic networks

Distinct engineered cell populations communicate and divide labor like a distributed computing system.

2040+

Germline-safe epigenetic medicine

Reversible expression-layer interventions reset disease states without permanent hereditary edits.

Design direction

Clinical framing first: indication-relevant biology, then mechanism, then modality.

The narrative is ordered like a translational briefing—surface the disease context and unmet need, then walk from measurable inputs (circulating proteins, tissue biomarkers, gene-expression signatures, metabolic flux) through decision logic that mimics how engineered circuits gate therapeutic output, and only then enumerate delivery modalities (mRNA, viral vectors, lipid nanoparticles, ex vivo–expanded cellular products). That sequencing mirrors how clinicians and reviewers weigh claims: plausibility of sensing, specificity of the conditional response, and a credible path from pharmacodynamics to patient-relevant endpoints.

Visually, each block is intended to carry the density of a methods-aware audience without sacrificing scanability: layered “stack” language parallels regulatory and IND-adjacent vocabulary (target product profile, critical quality attributes, control strategy). Research cards now encode three labeled fields—intervention class, biological rationale, and plausible indication anchor—as a fixed dl pattern. The Evidence section provides a figure panel slot, a CONSORT-aligned outcomes table scaffold, and a numbered citation layer so those assets can ship without reordering sections. Light and dark themes support extended reading of mechanistic copy; on-scroll reveals use short, low-amplitude motion so tables and pathway figures remain the visual focus when populated.
